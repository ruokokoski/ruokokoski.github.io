# AI Has Conquered Text and Images. Are Spreadsheets Next?

Most of the important data in the world does not look like a chatbot conversation or a photo. It looks like an Excel sheet: rows and columns filled with loan records, lab results, customer histories, and product quality and performance measurements. For a long time, the strongest tools for this kind of data have been machine learning methods built specifically for each table. In a recent seminar report, I looked at whether a newer kind of pretrained model, a tabular foundation model, can now do better. This blog post is a short summary of that comparison.

For the seminar report (the full report can be found [here](https://github.com/ruokokoski/tab-fm/blob/main/docs/ruokokoski2026_tabfm.pdf).), I compared two tabular foundation models, TabPFN-v2.5 and TabICL, against four strong baseline methods: Random Forest, XGBoost, CatBoost, and LightGBM. I tested them on three real datasets of different sizes: a small radar dataset (Ionosphere), a medium-sized White Wine Quality dataset, and a much larger Default of Credit Card Clients dataset. I measured both predictive performance and computational cost.

The main result is simple: the tabular foundation models achieved the best overall performance on all three datasets. That is the central finding. A single pretrained model was able to outperform strong classical methods even after those baseline models had been tuned separately for each dataset. That suggests something important may be changing. Table prediction may be moving toward the same kind of reusable pretrained systems that have already transformed language and image tasks.

At the same time, this was not a dramatic victory. The gains were usually small. On the easiest Inospehere dataset, almost every method performed very well, so there was limited room for one model to stand out. On the wine dataset, TabICL showed the clearest advantage. On the largest credit default dataset, the tabular foundation models still performed best, but only by a narrow margin. So the message is not that older methods have suddenly become obsolete. It is that these newer pretrained models are now strong enough to compete seriously, and sometimes win, without any extra work.

That ease of use is a large part of why this matters. In the usual workflow for tabular machine learning, you choose a model, tune many hyperparameters, compare several versions, and hope the search pays off. Tabular foundation models reduce some of that burden. In my comparison, they worked as strong plug-and-play options. That makes them especially attractive when you want a solid baseline quickly, when you are evaluating many datasets, or when you do not have the time or resources for repeated tuning. Time is money.

But there is also a clear downside. These models were much slower at prediction time on larger datasets. On the biggest dataset in my comparison, they needed about 50 to 60 seconds on gaming GPU just to produce predictions. The classical tree-based methods were much faster once trained. On CPU, the slowdown was even more severe, to the point that datasets with only a few thousand rows were already becoming impractical.

That trade-off makes the practical picture fairly clear. If your goal is to get strong results quickly on a small or medium-sized tabular problem, tabular foundation models are now very attractive. They are well suited for early prototypes, benchmarking, and research workflows where simplicity matters. But if you need low-latency predictions, high-throughput scoring, or efficient deployment on modest hardware, the older tree-based methods are still the safer choice.

So the main takeaway is not that spreadsheets have suddenly been solved by AI. It is that a new approach has become genuinely credible. In my seminar-report experiments, tabular foundation models were the most accurate overall, but they still paid a substantial price in speed. That is what makes this area interesting right now: the core idea already works, and the main remaining limitation is computational. If inference becomes faster, tabular foundation models could become a default choice for many everyday machine learning tasks built on rows and columns.
